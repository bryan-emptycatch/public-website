Seams, coupling, and the Law of Demeter

Jan 14, 2025

LoD isn't proof that coupling is evil. It's a boundary tool. The skill is choosing where your seams go.

The unofficial rule you pick up from classes, slides, and refactoring talks: don’t couple. Low coupling good, high coupling bad — simple story.

Then you hear “don’t talk to strangers” and “don’t chain your dots” — usually with order.getCustomer().getAddress().getPostalCode() as the villain — and it slots right into that same mindset.

That’s the wrong emphasis. You’re going to couple. Software that does anything useful is coupled. The real question is where you draw the line.

Coupling isn’t bad. Unowned coupling is.

Two pieces that work together are coupled. That’s not a moral failure — that’s how programs work.

What hurts is coupling you didn’t mean: code on this side of the system suddenly knows the internal shape of something on that side, and nobody ever agreed that was the boundary.

The goal isn’t to maximize looseness everywhere. It’s to choose your modules and your seams on purpose: here we want a stable surface; inside here we’re allowed to be dense and chatty and refactor freely.

Law of Demeter fits after that decision. It’s a way to enforce “we said this is the door; don’t crawl through the window.”

What LoD actually says

The slogan is “don’t talk to strangers.” The official object form is more specific: in a method, you only message certain collaborators — this, parameters, direct parts, objects you create, pragmatic globals.

It’s really about which dependencies a method takes on — especially reach-through dependencies that drag in types and navigation paths you never explicitly chose.

What it’s actually buying you

When you have a seam, LoD helps enforce structure hiding: don’t let callers skip the abstraction you put at the boundary. If Order is your module’s public face, the outside world probably shouldn’t walk order → customer → address to get a zip code — unless you decided that graph is the contract.

Inside a cohesive module, some density is fine. The law is relative to the seam, not absolute.

The dot chain is a boundary signal

order.getCustomer().getAddress().getPostalCode() is a path. It tells you the caller is depending on how a bunch of objects nest — not on an operation you chose to expose at a seam.

If that chain stays inside a module you own, it might just be your internal factoring. Fine.

If it starts on one side of a seam and dives through another team’s internals — that’s the expensive kind of coupling. Not because coupling exists, but because the line was never drawn, or never enforced.

Wrapper explosion is the cost of fighting the wrong fight

Wikipedia is honest about this: strict Demeter everywhere produces wrapper explosion — piles of tiny forwarding methods that add ceremony without adding clarity.

That’s what happens when you treat LoD as “minimize coupling in every method” instead of “honor the seams we picked.”

Be picky at the seam; stay practical inside the module.

Picky = deliberate about where the module line is and what crosses it. Practical = inside that line, don’t make yourself miserable satisfying a slogan.

What working with seams looks like

When I’m reviewing code, the questions I’m asking:

  • Did we mean for this to be a seam? If not, the fix might not be “add a wrapper” — it might be “redraw the boundary so this code lives inside the same module.”
  • If it is a seam: are we depending on their public story, or are we reaching through into their graph?
  • When they refactor behind the seam, should we break? If yes, own it as a real contract. If no, stop reaching through.

Sometimes the answer is a clear API on the other side. Sometimes it’s injecting the collaborator you actually need. Sometimes it’s moving code so the “wrong” coupling becomes internal coupling — which is allowed to be tight.

The short version

Law of Demeter is still useful. Not as “coupling is bad.” As: don’t accidentally weld your code to someone else’s internals — especially across a seam you never chose.

Be picky at the seam. Stay practical inside the module.
